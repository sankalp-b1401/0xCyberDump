Networking

Any set of things that are interconnected form a network. For instance, consider a group of friends: they can share resources, communicate, give and take amongst each other - so does the devices that are connected to a digital network.

  • ARPANET(Advanced Research Projects Agency Network) was the first wide area packet-switched network constructed in 1969 to transmit sensitive military data and to connect leading research groups throughout the United States.[1][2]

  • In 1989, Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web. He also created the HTML, the URL system and the HTTP. In short, he created the internet as we know. [3][4]

Internet

  1. Internet is the network of networks.
  2. It is a global network of interconnected computers and devices that allows users to access and share information and devices, allowing them to communicate and exchange data. It enables users to access a wide range of services, such as websites, emails, social media, online applications, and cloud storage.
  3. Internet is a public network.

Types of Network

  1. PAN - Personal Area Network
  2. LAN - Local Area Network
  3. CAN - Campus Area Network
  4. MAN - Metropolitan Area Network
  5. WAN - Wide Area Network
Network Type Concept Range Max Number of Hosts Advantages Disadvantages Technologies Used Example
PAN (Personal Area Network) A network centered around an individual for personal device connection Typically within 10 meters 5-10 devices Easy to set up, low cost, highly mobile Limited range, low data transfer rates, security concerns Bluetooth, Infrared, ZigBee, USB Connecting smartphone, smartwatch, and wireless earbuds
LAN (Local Area Network) A network covering a small geographic area like a home, office, or school Up to ~1 km Typically up to 1,000 hosts, but can scale High speed, low latency, inexpensive for small areas, easier maintenance Limited to a small geographic area, can become congested with many devices Ethernet, Wi-Fi, Token Ring Home Wi-Fi, Office network
CAN (Campus Area Network) Interconnects multiple LANs within a campus like universities or business parks Typically up to 10 km Thousands of hosts Connects multiple buildings efficiently, cost-effective for large organizations Limited to campus, not suited for larger geographical areas Fiber optics, Ethernet, Wi-Fi University network connecting departments and libraries
MAN (Metropolitan Area Network) Covers a city or large campus, connecting multiple LANs and CANs Up to ~50 km Tens of thousands of hosts High-speed connections over a large area, interconnects multiple sites More expensive, requires more management, potential for higher latency than LAN Fiber optics, wireless bridges, MPLS City-wide public Wi-Fi, cable TV networks
WAN (Wide Area Network) Spans large geographical areas, often country or continent-wide Unlimited (Global) Millions of hosts Connects remote locations, facilitates global communication Expensive, complex management, high latency compared to LANs MPLS, leased lines, satellites, VPNs, Internet backbone technologies The Internet, multinational corporate networks
